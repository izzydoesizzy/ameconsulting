The BOLD™ Method

A proprietary coaching and leadership framework grounded in three decades of executive experience navigating the complex dynamics of power, identity, and belonging.

“Under pressure, leaders don’t rise to the moment — they fall back on who they’ve already practiced being.”

The Framework

The BOLD™ Method emerged from Aileen’s 30+ years of lived experience leading as a Black woman in Canadian healthcare — navigating boardrooms, building teams, managing crises, and ultimately choosing to leave the C-suite to help others lead with more courage and less compromise.

It’s not a personality assessment or a leadership model borrowed from a textbook. It’s a practical framework for the work of becoming who you need to be before the pressure hits.

B

Becoming

Shedding the stories that keep you small

Before you can lead others boldly, you have to unlearn the narratives that taught you to shrink. Becoming is the foundational work of examining the stories — about worth, about belonging, about what “leadership” is supposed to look like — that have shaped how you show up.

For many leaders, especially women and women of color in corporate environments, these stories were internalized early: don’t be too much, don’t take up space, earn your seat by proving you deserve to be here. Becoming is the process of examining those narratives, deciding which ones serve you, and releasing the rest.

In Practice

  • ◆ Identity mapping: Who were you told to be vs. who you actually are?
  • ◆ Unlearning the “good leader” template that doesn’t fit
  • ◆ Recognizing code-switching and its costs
  • ◆ Building awareness of internalized biases about your own authority

In Practice

  • ◆ CliftonStrengths assessment to identify and leverage natural talents
  • ◆ Setting and communicating boundaries as a leadership practice
  • ◆ Negotiating compensation, titles, and scope from a place of worth
  • ◆ Executive presence that is authentic, not performative
O

Owning

Claiming authority and worth without apology

Owning is about closing the gap between what you know you’re worth and how you show up in the room. It’s the work of claiming your authority — your expertise, your contributions, your right to lead — without over-explaining, over-qualifying, or waiting for someone else to validate it.

This is where many high-achieving leaders get stuck. They have the credentials, the track record, the results — but something keeps them from fully stepping into their power. Owning addresses the internal barriers to claiming what you’ve earned.

L

Leading

Influencing under pressure and building cultures of trust

Leading is where the inner work meets the outer demands. This pillar focuses on the daily practice of showing up with influence, clarity, and accountability — especially when the stakes are high and the politics are real.

It’s about building the kind of leadership presence that creates psychological safety for your team while navigating the organizational dynamics that come with any complex workplace. This is where Crucial Conversations training, emotional intelligence development, and strategic relationship-building converge.

In Practice

  • ◆ Crucial Conversations methodology for high-stakes dialogue
  • ◆ Emotional intelligence development and 360 feedback integration
  • ◆ Building psychological safety in teams you lead
  • ◆ Navigating organizational politics with integrity

In Practice

  • ◆ Challenging organizational norms that perpetuate exclusion
  • ◆ Building cultures of accountability, not compliance
  • ◆ Mentoring the next generation of leaders who don’t fit the mold
  • ◆ Creating systemic change, not just personal advancement
D

Disrupting

Redesigning what leadership looks like

Disrupting is the most advanced and most necessary stage. It’s the commitment to not just succeed within existing systems, but to reshape them. Leaders who reach this stage are no longer content to fit into models of leadership that weren’t designed for them — they’re actively creating new ones.

This pillar is deeply connected to DEIA work because true disruption means questioning who gets to lead, what “professional” really means, and whether your organization’s culture is built for belonging or for performance theater.
